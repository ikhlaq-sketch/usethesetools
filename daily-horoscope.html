A Brief History of Horoscopes

The practice of astrology dates back to ancient Babylon (c. 2000 BCE), where priests observed celestial patterns to predict seasonal changes and royal events. The first personal horoscopes appeared in Hellenistic Egypt around 150 BCE, combining Babylonian planetary omens with Greek philosophy. The modern “daily horoscope” as we know it emerged in the 20th century with newspaper columns – particularly the work of R.H. Naylor (1930s) and later Linda Goodman (1960s). Today, millions of people read horoscopes daily, using them as a prompt for positive thinking and personal reflection.

How to Make the Most of Your Daily Horoscope

Read your horoscope in the morning, then set one small intention based on the message. For example, if your career horoscope suggests networking, reach out to one colleague. If it highlights self‑care, schedule a short break. Use the predictions as gentle prompts rather than rigid rules. Over time, you’ll notice patterns that help you understand your own reactions and strengths.
